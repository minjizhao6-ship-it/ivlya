IVLYA | The Story of E-commerce Supplies That Started With a DTC Brand

IVLYA began with real DTC operating problems. Today, we help e-commerce brands make every shipment more stable, every package more professional, and every customer experience more trustworthy.

It All Started With a DTC Brand Plan

Before IVLYA became an e-commerce supplies brand, our original idea was not to sell packaging materials or warehouse consumables.

Like many e-commerce founders, we wanted to build a DTC brand of our own.

We imagined a brand with a clear position, a professional website, quality product images, stable traffic, and customers who would come back again and again.

So we began the journey that many DTC founders go through.

We studied the market.

What products were people looking for? Who would buy them? Why would customers choose a brand they had never heard of before? How could a new brand earn its first layer of trust?

Then came product selection, photography, copywriting, website building, product page optimization, shipping settings, and advertising tests.

At the beginning, most of our attention was on the front end of the business.

Did the website look professional? Were the product images attractive enough? Could the ads get clicks? Could the product page convert visitors into customers? Was the price competitive? Would customers feel confident enough to place an order?

All of these things mattered.

In e-commerce, customers cannot touch your product or meet you in person. Your website, images, copy, and ads become the first way they understand your brand.

But once we entered real daily operations, we slowly realized something important.

A DTC brand does not run smoothly only because the website looks good or the ads perform well.

Behind every order, there are many details hidden in the back office, the warehouse, the printer, the shelves, and the packing table.

Thermal labels. Shipping mailers. Poly bags. Carton boxes. Packing tape. Labeling tools. Warehouse supplies.

A thermal label may look like just a piece of paper, but every package needs one. The shipping address, barcode, logistics information, and order details all rely on that label to move through the fulfillment system.

A shipping mailer may look like just a bag, but when the customer receives the order, the first thing they often see is not the product. It is the package.

A carton box may look like just a shipping container, but its size, strength, and protection directly affect transportation, damage risk, and shipping cost.

Packing tape, inner bags, fillers, label tools, and other warehouse supplies may not be the most visible part of a brand, but every order depends on them.

From the moment a customer places an order, to the moment the warehouse prints the label, picks the product, packs it, seals it, labels it, scans it, and hands it over to the carrier, e-commerce supplies are involved in every step.

At first, we did not realize how important these small materials were.

We thought e-commerce was mainly about products, traffic, and conversion rates.

Later, we understood something deeper.

Products explain why customers buy. Traffic helps customers find you. Conversion rate shows whether they are willing to place an order. But fulfillment and packaging decide whether customers will still trust you after receiving the product.

E-commerce supplies are not just supporting materials. They are the foundation of order fulfillment, and they are also part of the brand experience.

The First Time We Realized Supplies Were Not a Small Detail

What changed our view was not a cost sheet or a supplier quotation. It was the small problems that happened again and again during real order fulfillment.

At the beginning, in order to control costs, we did what many early-stage sellers do. We treated supplies as an area where costs should be reduced as much as possible.

Cheaper thermal labels should still print. Cheaper shipping mailers should still ship orders. Packaging materials are only supporting items. The product itself is what matters. As long as the product is good, customers probably will not care too much about the outer packaging.

This thinking is very common.

Especially when a DTC brand is just starting, every cost matters. Advertising costs money. Building a website costs money. Samples cost money. Inventory costs money. Logistics costs money.

Compared with these expenses, labels, mailers, boxes, and tape seem like small costs.

But after real operations began, we realized that the small costs we tried to save often came back in another form.

Once, a batch of orders was ready. The products had been picked. The packages had been prepared. The only thing left was to print the shipping labels, apply them, and hand the packages over to the carrier.

But the labels came out too light. The barcode edges looked slightly blurred.

At first glance, they were not completely unusable. The address was visible. The name was visible. The barcode was there. So we did not think much of it.

But during scanning, the problem appeared.

Some labels scanned immediately. Some labels needed to be tilted and scanned several times. Some labels could not be recognized at all.

The warehouse team had to pull those packages aside, reprint the labels, apply them again, and recheck the orders.

A batch of shipments that should have gone out smoothly was slowed down by something as ordinary as a label.

A thermal label is not just a piece of paper. It connects the order system, the warehouse process, and the logistics network.

Later, we also experienced adhesive problems.

Some labels looked fine right after being applied. The edges were flat and everything seemed normal. But after some time, the corners started to lift.

This became more noticeable when packages went through handling, stacking, friction, or temperature changes during transportation.

The difficult part was that this problem did not always appear immediately. In the warehouse, everything looked normal. When the package was handed to the carrier, it still looked fine. But once it entered the shipping process, the risk began.

If the label lifted, the barcode could be damaged. If the label came off, the package information could become unreadable. If the shipment was delayed, the customer would not know it was caused by a label issue.

The customer would only feel: this brand is not professional enough; this package does not look reliable; this shopping experience does not feel safe.

For a DTC brand, this kind of trust loss is dangerous.

Customers do not separate the problem into poor label quality, unstable adhesive, or shipping environment changes. They connect the whole experience to the brand.

When the package looks good, the brand feels professional. When the package looks bad, the brand feels unreliable.

Shipping mailers taught us another lesson.

At first, we thought a mailer only needed to hold the product. But after shipping real orders, we realized that the sealing strength, thickness, size, and texture of a mailer all affect the customer's first impression.

For example, a clothing brand may have beautiful product images, a clean website, and a strong visual style. But if the customer receives a wrinkled mailer with a weak seal or slightly torn edges, the first impression is already damaged before the product is even seen.

The product has not appeared yet, but the brand experience has already lost points.

For jewelry, handmade products, or higher-value items, if the packaging is too thin and becomes deformed during shipping, the customer may feel that the brand did not handle the order with enough care, even if the product itself is not damaged.

To customers, packaging is not a hidden material. Packaging is part of the delivery experience.

It tells the customer: Is this brand careful? Is this brand professional? Does this brand care about my order?

Packaging size also became an important issue.

When order volume is low, inconsistent packaging does not seem like a major problem. Use this mailer today and another box tomorrow. Pack this product one way and that product another way. Let the warehouse team decide based on experience.

At a small scale, this may work. But when order volume grows, inconsistency quickly becomes an efficiency problem.

Which mailer should be used for this product? Is this box too large? Should filler be added? Where should the label be placed? Should the same SKU always use the same packaging? Do different sales channels require different labels or carton requirements?

Every temporary decision takes time. Every mismatched package can create waste.

If the mailer is too large, more filler may be needed. If the box is too large, shipping volume may increase. If the package is too small, the product may be squeezed. If label placement is not consistent, warehouse scanning and carrier handling can become less efficient.

Many of these problems do not become complaints on the same day. But they happen every day.

A few extra minutes wasted every day. A few more reworks in every batch. A little more packaging waste each month. A little more uncertainty in every shipment.

These small losses may not look serious in the short term. But over time, they become real costs.

The most alarming issue was supply instability.

Many sellers face this situation at the beginning: the labels purchased today work well, but the next batch prints differently; the mailers used this month seal properly, but the next batch from another supplier feels weaker; a size that the team has already built a process around suddenly goes out of stock; a familiar packing process has to be adjusted again because the supplies changed.

This kind of uncertainty is more painful than a slightly higher unit cost.

Because in e-commerce operations, the biggest fear is not that one material costs a little more. The bigger fear is not knowing whether fulfillment will be smooth tomorrow.

Will the labels print clearly? Will the mailers seal properly? Will the same specification still be available next time? Will the supplies keep up when order volume increases?

All of this uncertainty eventually becomes operational pressure.

These experiences made us truly understand: supplies are not a small detail.

They are not supporting items that can be considered only after the order is complete. From the moment fulfillment begins, they are already part of the customer experience.

A clear and stable label, a securely sealed mailer, and a properly sized packaging solution may look ordinary, but they determine whether an order can ship smoothly and how the customer feels when the package arrives.

After that, we stopped treating e-commerce supplies as simple low-cost purchasing items. We started to see them as a basic but essential part of the DTC operating system.

The Real Cost of E-commerce Supplies Is Not Just the Purchase Price

Many sellers focus on unit price at the beginning.

A roll of labels that is a little cheaper. A mailer that costs a few cents less. A carton box that saves a little money. A roll of tape that is slightly cheaper.

On the surface, it looks like cost saving.

This thinking is not wrong. Every business needs to control costs, especially in e-commerce.

Ad costs are rising. Platform fees are rising. Shipping costs are rising. Return costs are rising. Sellers naturally want to save wherever possible.

But after entering real fulfillment, we learned this: the real cost of e-commerce supplies is not only the purchase price. It is the stability cost during actual use.

Take thermal labels as an example.

On the surface, the difference between two labels may only be a small price gap per roll.

But if the cheaper label has a higher failure rate, everything changes.

One failed print means printing again. One unclear barcode means relabeling. One label coming off can lead to shipping delays. One unreadable package may create customer service work and customer dissatisfaction.

At that point, the money saved on purchase price may already be lost through rework, labor, and after-sales costs.

The same is true for shipping mailers.

A mailer that saves a few cents may look attractive. But if the seal is weak, the material is too thin, or the package is more likely to tear during shipping, the customer's experience suffers. If it leads to refund requests, replacements, or complaints, the real loss is far more than a few cents.

Customers do not care how much you saved on packaging. They only judge the experience when the package arrives.

Does this brand feel trustworthy? Does this package feel safe? Does this shopping experience feel professional?

The same applies to boxes and packaging specifications.

An unsuitable box can increase dimensional shipping cost. An oversized package may require more filler. An undersized package may squeeze and damage the product. An unstandardized packaging process may force warehouse staff to make repeated decisions every day.

These costs do not appear on the purchase order. They are hidden inside daily operations.

Some are labor costs. Some are packaging waste. Some are shipping costs. Some are customer complaints. Some are lost brand trust.

Many times, sellers think they are saving on purchasing, but they are simply moving the cost into later operational stages.

A little is saved at the beginning. More time, more communication, and more after-sales work are spent later.

For a growing DTC brand, the most frightening thing is not that one supply item costs a little more. It is the uncertainty of daily fulfillment.

Today the labels print well, tomorrow they do not. Today the mailers seal firmly, tomorrow they do not. Today the packaging size works, tomorrow it has to be adjusted. Today orders ship on time, tomorrow shipping is delayed because supplies are out of stock.

This uncertainty makes it difficult for a team to build a stable process. And a brand without a stable process is difficult to scale.

When order volume is small, problems can be solved manually. Check one more time. Print one more label. Change one more mailer. Explain one more time to the customer.

But when order volume grows, every small problem becomes larger.

At ten orders a day, two failed labels are just a small inconvenience. At one hundred orders a day, the same failure rate becomes a visible efficiency loss. At one thousand orders a day, any instability can become a system-level risk.

This was a key turning point for IVLYA.

True value is not the lowest unit price. True value means reducing errors, rework, waste, and uncertainty over long-term use.

A label that prints reliably is more important than one that is simply cheap. A mailer that seals securely is more important than the lowest price. A packaging solution that fits the product and shipping method is more important than random purchasing. A stable supply relationship is more important than finding a new supplier every time.

For e-commerce sellers, supplies are not finished after purchase. They participate in fulfillment every day. And every day, they affect cost, efficiency, and customer experience.
